Artifact 1 Reflection — CareerPath AI
Why I chose this
I selected this artifact because it shows I can build a complete AI product from scratch — not just use tools, but design a user experience around them. Most students in this class used AI; I built with it.
What I learned
The biggest lesson was that AI quality is a design problem, not a model problem. The chatbot's usefulness came almost entirely from how I structured the prompts and defined the system role — not from the underlying model itself.
What I would improve
The bot currently gives general advice rather than truly personalized guidance. If I continued, I'd add memory and user profiling so the bot remembers past conversations and builds a tailored career roadmap over time.

💡 Why this happened: Each version added a new layer of context — first the persona, then the domain, then the format. The AI has no memory of your intent; it can only respond to what you give it. More context = fewer assumptions = better output.
⚔️
Experiment 2 — Cross-Model Patterns
Same prompt, different systems — what changed and why
What stayed the same across all models
✓ Career roles (Data Analyst, ML Engineer, BI Specialist)
✓ Core skills (Python, SQL, Statistics)
✓ Advice to pursue projects and internships
What differed — and why
Depth of explanation
Models with larger context windows produced more detailed job descriptions. This reflects training data differences, not intelligence differences.
Response structure
Some models defaulted to bullet lists; others wrote paragraphs. The prompt didn't specify format — so each model applied its own RLHF-trained style preference.
Certification emphasis
One model heavily emphasized certifications. This likely reflects more certification-focused content in its training data, not necessarily better advice.
💡 Pattern discovered: Model differences are mostly stylistic, not substantive. The same information appeared across all models — but packaged differently. This means prompt format matters more than which model you pick for informational tasks.
🔍
Experiment 3 — Context vs. No Context
Identical question, different input framing
❌ Without Context
"What jobs should I apply for?"
Suggested generic roles: teacher, manager, engineer, analyst. No specific skills mentioned. Not actionable for anyone.
★★★★★

★★★★★
5/5 Relevance
💡 Why this matters: LLMs are not search engines — they generate based on probability. Without context, the model defaults to the most statistically common answer. Adding personal background forces the model to narrow its output distribution to your specific situation.

Experiment 4 — AI Research Assistant
Aggregation speed vs. critical thinking quality
The experiment
I used an AI research assistant to gather information on technology career trends — then compared the quality and speed to manual searching across LinkedIn, Indeed, and Google.
~5×
Faster aggregation — AI gathered & structured in 2 min vs. 10+ min manually
⚠️
Lower source verification — AI didn't cite sources; manual search allowed direct validation
What AI did well
✓ Synthesized information from multiple domains into one structured output
✓ Organized results with clear headings and hierarchy automatically
✓ Identified patterns across job postings faster than human scanning
What AI couldn't replace
✗ Evaluating whether a source is trustworthy or current
✗ Distinguishing between general trend and local market reality
💡 Core insight: AI research tools are accelerators, not replacements. The real skill is knowing what to ask, how to verify, and when to trust the output — which requires human judgment that the AI itself lacks.
Artifact 2 Reflection — AI Lab Experiments
Why I chose this
This artifact shows a fundamentally different skill from Artifact 1. Rather than building something, I studied something — treating AI as a system to be understood, not just used. That analytical ability is what separates a practitioner from a power user.
What surprised me
I expected different AI models to produce meaningfully different career advice. They didn't — the content was nearly identical across all models. What differed was formatting and depth. This told me that prompt design matters far more than model selection for informational tasks.
What challenged me
The hardest part was moving from "something changed" to "here is why it changed." It's easy to notice that context-rich prompts worked better. It's harder to explain that this happens because LLMs generate based on probability distributions narrowed by context. That explanation required deeper research.

Artifact 4 Reflection — AI Tools Comparison
Why I chose this
This artifact demonstrates strategic evaluation — a skill distinct from building (Artifact 1), experimenting (Artifact 2), or researching (Artifact 3). Knowing which tool to use, and why, is one of the most practical AI skills an employer can hire for in 2026.
What I learned
The biggest surprise was how little model quality varies for common tasks. Most tools return similar results for writing and career advice. The real differences emerge at the extremes: complex reasoning, live search, and STEM tasks — where specialized tools pull far ahead.
What I would improve
I would add real benchmark citations (MMLU, HumanEval, LMSYS Chatbot Arena scores) to support the scores with published data, and build a score-update mechanism so the dashboard stays current as models release new versions throughout 2026.
